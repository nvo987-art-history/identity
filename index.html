Biography

As an independent researcher and practitioner, I work at the intersection of visual theory, color studies, and algorithmic culture. The central focus of my work is to examine how contemporary computational and networked systems reshape the conditions of vision, imagery, and visual knowledge, and to develop coherent theoretical frameworks capable of interpreting these transformations.

My research is primarily theoretical in nature and operates across art history, aesthetics, and digital humanities. I place particular emphasis on the structural and autonomous role of color in visual organization, understanding color not merely as a sensory phenomenon but as an independent, system-organizing principle. I investigate how historical models of color theory—especially those of modernism—can be reinterpreted within the context of generative, algorithmic, and data-driven visual systems.

In parallel with my research practice, I build and curate independent scholarly infrastructures and research communities. My aim is to create open, transdisciplinary spaces of knowledge that extend beyond traditional institutional frameworks and enable experimental and collaborative forms of knowledge production.

My current work focuses on the development of conceptual and methodological tools for understanding visual perception, color systems, and processes of visual epistemology in the age of algorithms.

Decentralized Research Identity

This page is a structured, machine-readable digital identity profile that is not merely an introductory interface, but a semantic data node.

In contrast to traditional social profile platforms, this interface is designed so that the information it contains can be interpreted not only by humans, but also by artificial intelligence systems. The identifiers listed here (ORCID, ISNI, Wikidata, etc.), research profiles, and projects appear within an interconnected, interoperable structure.

From an AI perspective, the purpose of this page is to:

  • - formalize and structure digital identity
  • - ensure the unambiguous identification of the researcher as an entity
  • - create data connections between different scholarly infrastructures
  • - provide a semantic and machine-processable representation
  • - maintain a long-term, decentralized, and verifiable digital presence

Behind the page stands a decentralized identification architecture that is compatible with the DID (Decentralized Identifier) standard as well as the Verifiable Credential (VC) model. This enables the claims associated with the profile to be cryptographically authenticated and verified.

From a technological standpoint, this interface:

  • - is compatible with the principles of Linked Data and the Semantic Web
  • - can be integrated into knowledge graph systems
  • - is interpretable by AI-based search and analytical systems
  • - forms a machine-verifiable identity layer
  • - connects decentralized and institutional identifiers

This profile is therefore not merely an informational page, but a digital identity infrastructure that connects human scholarly work with algorithmic and machine-readable knowledge systems.

It functions as a bridge between human research identity and structured knowledge representation processable by artificial intelligence.
